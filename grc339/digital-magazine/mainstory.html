The Enduring Popularity of Boba

Mango green tea, regular ice, 30% sugar, with — most importantly — boba. More than just my classic order, during the past few months, it became almost a mantra. Throughout a grueling spring semester, I drew motivation from an unexpected source: the boba I bought myself after work every Friday. Often, this became the highlight of my week. Some days a friend accompanied me; other times, I frequented Tea-Do or Boba King alone. Either way, the effects were clear. After the first sip, my tension dissipated. A rush of happiness — sometimes the first I’d felt in days — permeated my body, the tangy flavor of mango dancing on my tongue. Even the simple act of carrying my drink across campus, sipping intermittently — trying to savor each pearl’s subtle sweetness — was a transformative one. Every face I passed belonged no longer to an impassive stranger, but a potential friend, linked to me by the cup in my hand.

I am immensely grateful for the privilege to be able to treat myself in this way, as the price of even the cheapest boba is an unjustifiable expense for many. Still, in recent years, its prevalence in America has only seemed to increase.

A brief history of boba, also known as bubble tea, reveals its origins in Taiwan. While its roots trace back to the development of shou yao during WWII, the drink assumed its current form in the late 1980s, when two different tea houses — Chun Shui Tang and Hanlin Tea Room — claim to have concurrently invented it by adding black tapioca balls to milk tea (the dispute over the “true” innovator still remains to be unsettled). Since then, boba has spread worldwide to areas with significant Asian populations. The options, too, expanded, from the classic milk tea to fruit tea and brown sugar boba, often featuring a range of customizable options and choices. Although this sweet treat only recently entered the American mainstream, it has enjoyed consistent popularity among Asians and Asian Americans for decades — with good reason.

While not exactly scientific, my firsthand experience (and that of many of my friends) points to boba as a reliable source of stress relief and social connection. The high sugar content (although not always the healthiest) can immediately boost one’s mood, while my mother theorizes that munching on firm, chewy pearls releases the body’s built-up tension. When consumed alone, these benefits may fade soon after the last sip — yet boba can also facilitate bonding within a community, providing longer-lasting perks. In many East Asian American spaces, the question “What’s your go-to boba order?” has become a classic line, breaking down barriers and inviting connection (or a friendly debate over the ideal percentage of sugar). Furthermore, drinking boba is often a social event, enjoyed with friends or a date. The Fung Brothers’ viral 2013 music video “Bobalife” depicts young, stylish Asians sipping boba on the go or catching up at a sit-down tea house after work, a practice that has continued in the decade since the song’s release. For many Asian Americans, boba is more than just a drink. It is an essential way to combat loneliness in an increasingly isolating world, drawing us closer to others like ourselves.

Of course, to claim that boba alone can transcend cultural boundaries would be reductive, since not every Asian American grew up with or enjoys the drink. As Mochi staff writer Kristin Lee points out, boba is primarily an East Asian phenomenon, one that fails to unite the vast diversity that exists within Asian America. Having traveled from Iowa to the East Coast for college, she was unfamiliar with boba, sharing that it “made [her] feel like an outsider.” Being Asian or Asian American does not guarantee some sort of genetic affinity for boba; to suggest such a thing would veer into racist stereotyping that Asians have fought so long against.
